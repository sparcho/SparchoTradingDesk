1. EXECUTIVE FRAMING

India's energy sector sits at the intersection of three structural forces: (1) persistent demand-supply deficit that compounds the backlog year-on-year (deepened in §4); (2) policy-mandated renewable transition with 500 GW non-fossil-fuel target by 2030; (3) private-capital cycle re-acceleration as listed players raise equity at premium PE multiples to fund capacity additions. The investable surface spans 10 sub-segments (A-J in §3; v1 had 9, v2 adds Wind Power).

Macro overlay (carry from v1 + 260502 macro update): YELLOW regime per current MACRO_REGIME read; DXY 97.93 (silver-supportive but also INR-supportive secondary effect on imported energy components); Brent $108.96 above $105 trigger (FIRED — affects diesel-generation gen-cost + imported PV cell cost). UAE-OPEC exit eff. 1-May 2026 introduces 12-24 month medium-term oil supply softening probability — bullish for India's energy-import bill if it materializes.

Global comparator (Rajiv pillar 4 — partial coverage; full §6 deferred to v3): US power-grid buildout has hit interconnection-queue constraints (PJM 5+ year wait; ERCOT supply auctions spiking). EU energy-transition is policy-mature but capital-constrained. China is dominant on PV cell mfg (counter-tell: Longi/Jinko mass production of copper-substituted cells in Q2 2026 = silver-PV substitution risk for Indian solar capacity). India's relative position: power-grid headroom + skilled labour + favourable tariff regime + Make-in-India PLI for solar/battery cells = capital-attractive geography.

4. STRUCTURAL-DEFICIT FRAMING (NEW IN V2 per Rajiv pillar 5; §12 (d) compliance)

4.1 The YoY supply-demand gap

India's electricity demand growth has consistently outpaced supply additions:

  • Demand CAGR FY21-FY26: ~7-8% per CEA estimates (PENDING-OPERATOR-VERIFY current numbers)
  • Supply additions CAGR FY21-FY26: ~5-6% per CEA installed-capacity data
  • Implied YoY deficit: ~2-3% of demand each year compounds the structural backlog

4.2 Why this matters as a multi-year thesis (not a tactical setup)

A persistent gap is contractual revenue waiting to be converted. Companies positioned as "deficit-converters" benefit from sustained demand for capacity additions over the multi-year horizon — not single-quarter setups. This justifies paying compounder-class PE multiples (per Rajiv pillar 9: PE > 20 acceptable for proven moats with structural deficit tailwind).

4.4 Time horizon for deficit closure

Per CEA + IEEFA estimates, India needs ~700-800 GW total installed capacity by 2032 vs current ~470 GW = ~250-300 GW addition over 7-8 years at policy-target trajectory. Even at high-execution pace, this implies sustained capex demand for the multi-year compounder names through 2030+.

Upside catalyst: any acceleration of capex (e.g. data-centre demand surge from AI infra adds incremental electricity demand fastest); any drought-related hydro shortfall (forces faster renewable+thermal addition).

Downside catalyst: demand-side surprises (industrial slowdown, slower EV adoption); PV-substitution shock (if Longi/Jinko silver-free cells materially cheapen solar capex).
