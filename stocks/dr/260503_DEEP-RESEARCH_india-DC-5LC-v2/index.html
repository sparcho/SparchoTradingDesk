1. EXECUTIVE FRAMING

India is positioned to absorb 12-15% of global incremental data-centre capacity demand over 2026-2030. The driver is NOT just India's own AI/cloud adoption (still <3% of global compute spend) — it's that the alternative geographies for capacity expansion are constrained, and India has comparative slack on the binding constraints (power, land, labour cost, sovereign-data-routing requirement). v1 covered the listed-side India-DC value chain across 5LC layers; v2 adds the global comparator that anchors why India + the undersea cables foundational layer that makes the DC build-out physically viable.

The thesis arc:

  1. Global hyperscaler capex demand is structural multi-year (Microsoft + Meta + Google + Amazon + Oracle combined: $250-300B/yr capex run-rate in 2026, growing) — see §6
  2. Capex is colliding with US power-grid bottleneck (interconnection queues) + EU policy friction + China decoupling pressure
  3. India sits at the intersection: power-grid headroom (NTPC + Power Grid + renewable ramp), labour cost arbitrage, DPDP Act creating sovereign-data-routing demand, IRA-style PLI for chips/cells, favourable tariff regime
  4. Two foundational physical layers determine India's actual capacity to absorb the demand: (a) electricity (covered in 260428 Energy DR v2 — see deficit framing), (b) undersea fibre connectivity (NEW IN V2 — see §7)
  5. Listed India players capture the demand at multiple layers (5LC mapped in §3); global hyperscalers + unlisted private operators dominate the actual hyperscale build-out (§6 + §8)

4. STRUCTURAL-DEFICIT FRAMING (§12 (d))

Triple-deficit thesis for India DC:

  1. Power supply deficit (covered in detail in 260428 Energy DR v2 §4) — DC adds incremental electricity demand fastest of any sector; current grid headroom partial offset
  2. Connectivity deficit — India has only ~15-17 active undersea cables landing on its coasts vs Singapore's 25+ or US East Coast's 40+; bandwidth becomes a binding constraint for hyperscale workloads (see §7)
  3. Skilled-DC-operations talent deficit — global average is ~6-8 years to train a hyperscale DC ops engineer; India's pool is currently < 30,000 vs requirement of 100,000+ by 2030 if hyperscale demand materializes

The deficit-converters: capacity-builders (ANANTRAJ, BLUESTAR, POLYCAB, KEI), connectivity-providers (BHARTIARTL, STLTECH, TATA COMM), and adjacent (energy-side from Energy DR v2). All have multi-year contractual revenue waiting to be converted as deficit closes.

6.2 Why prefer India OR alternative geographies (the constraint pushing capital toward India)

US (the dominant historical destination) — constraints:

  • Power-grid bottleneck: PJM interconnection queue 5+ year wait for new gen capacity; ERCOT supply-auction prices spiking 30-50% YoY; new DC builds delayed 18-36 months waiting for grid hookup
  • IRA / permit moratorium friction: new gas-peaker plants face permit delays even where power demand exists
  • Water constraints in arid regions (Phoenix, parts of Texas) — chip cooling water requirement at hyperscale forces relocation to wetter geographies
  • Labour cost: $200K-$400K+ for hyperscale DC ops engineers; India equivalent $20-50K
  • NIMBY pushback: local-gov DC moratoriums in 6+ counties as of FY26

EU — constraints:

  • Energy-transition policy mature but capex-constrained; hyperscale build cost 1.5-2x India equivalent
  • Stricter data-residency rules; AI Act friction
  • Russia-EU energy supply uncertainty post-2022 ongoing
  • Labour cost ~$150-250K hyperscale DC ops

China — constraints:

  • US decoupling pressure on hyperscale chips export (HBM, advanced packaging) creates supply-chain risk for China DC build
  • Tencent + Alibaba + ByteDance dominate domestic DC; foreign hyperscalers blocked from significant local presence
  • Capital outflow restrictions discourage US/EU hyperscaler DC capex commitment

Singapore — constraints:

  • DC moratorium 2019-2022 (lifted 2022 with sustainability conditions); space-constrained physically
  • Cable-landing capacity already saturated
  • High power tariff vs India

Indonesia / Vietnam / Malaysia — constraints:

  • Faster regulatory + grid build-out pace recently but workforce supply still scaling
  • Regional sovereign-data competition with India

India — comparative advantages:

  • Power-grid headroom (Energy DR v2 deficit framing notes 700-800 GW target by 2032 = capex demand is structural)
  • Labour cost arbitrage (factor 5-10x cheaper hyperscale DC ops vs US)
  • DPDP Act creating sovereign-data-routing demand (Indian customer data must reside in India for many use cases)
  • PLI schemes for solar/battery cells partially de-risk power-supply ramp
  • English-speaking workforce + IT services backbone (TCS/INFOSYS) for hyperscaler operations partnerships
  • Geopolitical alignment with US (vs China-block) without being a US-allied dependency

India — comparative disadvantages:

  • Connectivity layer (undersea cables) is currently a bottleneck — see §7
  • Capex returns slower than US for premium services
  • DPDP Act creates compliance overhead (costly + uncertain)
  • Climate/water risks in inland DC locations (Hyderabad summer cooling cost)

The global capex-flow tailwind is multi-year + structural. Even at conservative 15-25% incremental India-share growth, the listed deficit-converters in §3 capture multi-year revenue tailwinds. Names with the cleanest hyperscale-capture path:

  • ANANTRAJ (land + build) — direct beneficiary; PE re-rating likely as DC partnerships materialize
  • BHARTIARTL (connectivity) — recurring revenue from hyperscaler bandwidth contracts
  • BLUESTAR (cooling) — every new hyperscale rack needs HVAC; volume play
  • POWERGRID (transmission backbone) — defensive monopoly capture
  • NTPC + ADANIGREEN + TATAPOWER (generation) — power-supply layer

7. UNDERSEA CABLES — foundational connectivity layer (NEW IN V2 — Rajiv's secondary ask)

7.1 Why undersea cables matter for DC

Hyperscale DCs require massive bandwidth to interconnect with global networks (cross-region replication, AI training data ingestion, content delivery). Undersea fibre cables carry ~99% of intercontinental data traffic — they're the literal physical foundation of the cloud. India's connectivity capacity is determined by:

  1. Number of cables landing on Indian coasts (currently ~15-17 vs Singapore's 25+, US East Coast's 40+)
  2. Landing station density (Mumbai dominates; Chennai second; Tuticorin/Cochin/Vishakapatnam tertiary)
  3. Capacity per cable (newer cables = 200-400 Tbps; older cables = 1-10 Tbps)
  4. Latency to key endpoints (Singapore = ~30ms; Tokyo = ~80ms; US West Coast = ~200ms; Frankfurt = ~120ms)

Why India has a connectivity deficit: historically under-invested; cable-laying announcements take 3-5 years from RFP to commissioning; landing-station permits stuck in bureaucratic queues.

7.4 Why this is a multi-year compounding thesis (§12 (d) compliance)

Each new cable adds 100-400 Tbps of capacity. Each capacity tranche unlocks DC build-out economics for inland sites that were previously bandwidth-constrained. Multi-year cable pipeline + multi-year DC build = sustained revenue tailwind for the connectivity-layer names. Can't easily be rushed (cable laying is physically slow); can't easily be substituted (no satellite-internet equivalent at hyperscale bandwidth).
