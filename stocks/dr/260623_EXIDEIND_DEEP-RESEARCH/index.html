4. Executive Summary

Exide Industries is India's largest lead-acid battery maker — a near-duopoly with Amara Raja across auto OEM, replacement, industrial, home-UPS and solar, plus a defensible defence/submarine franchise. The base business is cash-generative, debt-free and dividend-paying, but structurally low-growth (3y sales CAGR 6%, screener.in 23-Jun-2026) and low-return on a consolidated basis (ROCE 8.54%, ROE 5.97%, screener.in). The reason the consolidated returns look so much worse than the standalone (standalone ROCE 28.8%, scanx FY26) is Exide Energy Solutions (EES) — the wholly-owned Li-ion cell subsidiary into which the group has now sunk ₹4,802 Cr of equity (scanx, FY26 call) with no commercial revenue yet. Consolidated FY26 PAT was ₹859.92 Cr vs standalone ₹1,111 Cr (indiaipo.in / screener.in) — the ~₹250 Cr gap is, in essence, the EES start-up drag plus consolidation adjustments. The whole investment debate is whether that drag converts into a second growth engine.

CORE VERDICT — A debt-free incumbent paying ~39x for an unproven option. Light-filter: 3/8 pass (consolidated) / 4/8 standalone. The load-bearing question: does the Li-ion / energy-storage + defence-battery pivot (the EES giga-factory) become a material, well-capitalised growth engine — or is it capex-heavy optionality stacked on a low-ROCE auto-ancillary base? As of 23-Jun-2026 the market is paying a growth multiple (P/E ~39x cons.) for a business whose current returns and growth do not justify it — the entire gap is faith in the pivot.

Operator-relevant context, addressed head-on. The position (125 sh @ ₹396.16, HDFC book, T2; LTP ~₹380.75) is held for the pivot, not the base — operator-confirmed energy-storage theme (2026-06-04). That framing is the correct one: nobody should own Exide at ~39x consolidated earnings for a 6%-growth lead-acid franchise; you own it because you believe EES (Li-ion cells, BESS, defence) re-rates the whole entity. The held-card levels (avg 396 ≈ fib 3.618 ≈ resistance edge; invalidation daily-close <361 = SMA200) line up with where consensus analyst targets cluster (mean ~₹365–370, with a bull tail to ₹435–470 — see §5). The honest tension: standalone is a high-ROCE cash machine; consolidated is a low-ROCE entity because the cash machine is funding a pre-revenue cell plant. If EES delivers, today's "expensive" multiple is cheap optionality; if EES slips or stays sub-scale, the operator owns a 6%-grower at a premium multiple. This brief does not resolve that — it sharpens it.

5. Section 1 / Company Overview

Exide Industries Ltd (incorporated 1947, HQ Kolkata) is India's largest manufacturer of lead-acid storage batteries, with leadership across automotive (OEM + replacement), industrial (UPS, infra, telecom, solar) and a strategic defence/submarine franchise. It is now vertically extending into Li-ion cell manufacturing via its wholly-owned subsidiary Exide Energy Solutions Ltd (EES). FY26 consolidated revenue ₹17,995 Cr; standalone ₹17,269 Cr (screener.in / indiaipo.in, FY26).

7. Section 3 / The Li-ion / Energy-Storage + Defence-Battery Pivot — Real Engine or Capex Optionality?

This is the variant-perception axis — and where the held thesis lives. Consensus largely values Exide on the lead-acid base and treats EES as call-option upside; the bull (held) view is that EES is the real story and the base is the funding vehicle.

What the effort actually is. EES is building India's first large-scale Li-ion cell (not just pack-assembly) plant in Bengaluru. Cell manufacturing is the deep-technology, capital-intensive node most Indian players have skipped in favour of importing cells. Exide chose to climb the harder curve, supported by: a Hyundai–Kia LFP localisation partnership (demand anchor), a domestic-cost tailwind (Chinese cell-import VAT changes adding ~3% now, ~9% by Jan-2027 per management — scanx), and a separate, defensible defence-battery franchise (indigenous Scorpene submarine batteries replacing German imports, May-2026).

Honest scepticism — 5 open questions

  1. Revenue & profit, not just capacity. ₹4,802 Cr is in; commercial cell revenue is still ~zero. Until utilisation and yield are demonstrated at scale (not targeted), this is capex, not an engine.
  2. Margin & ROCE of cells vs the base. Cell manufacturing globally is brutally competitive (CATL/BYD scale, LFP price deflation). Will EES earn a return above its cost of capital, or dilute group ROCE for years?
  3. Customer concentration / commitment. A Hyundai–Kia partnership is a strong signal — but is it a binding off-take with volumes/prices, or a framework? Single-anchor risk is real (PENDING off-take detail).
  4. PLI absence. If Exide is building cells without the ACC-PLI subsidy that competitors (Reliance, others) may carry, its unit economics start at a structural disadvantage vs subsidised rivals. Verify against the latest IP — this is a thesis-critical PENDING.
  5. Capital cadence. ₹4,802 Cr in, ₹1,400 Cr planned FY27, and Phase-II (to 12 GWh) ahead — funded so far from internal cash + the HDFC Life monetisation. Will Phase-II require external capital / dilution, and at what cost?
PIVOT VERDICT — Genuine, credible, and the right strategic bet — but still optionality, not yet an engine. Exide is doing the hard version (cells, not packs), has a marquee anchor (Hyundai-Kia) and a defensible defence flank. But as of 23-Jun-2026 it is pre-revenue, possibly un-subsidised (PLI PENDING), and unproven on yield/ROCE at scale. The held thesis is intellectually sound; the risk is timing and returns, not direction. Falsification = utilisation/yield miss or no commercial revenue inflection through FY27 (see §11).

8. Section 4 / Core Auto-Ancillary Business Risk + Margins

Structural risk. (1) EV transition — long-run, ICE replacement batteries shrink; Exide's own EES build is the hedge against its own base. (2) Replacement vs OEM mix — the higher-margin replacement aftermarket (~70% of auto) is the profit anchor; OEM is volume/low-margin. (3) Commodity cost — the dominant near-term swing: sulphur prices surged ~5x YoY (₹15→₹74–75/kg), a ~₹150 Cr negative hit in Q4 FY26; lead (LME ~1,950) softened YoY but ~10% rupee depreciation offset it; polypropylene also rose (scanx, FY26). Exide passed ~5–6% price hikes (Jan/Mar/Apr) with a one-quarter lag on OEM contracts.

What's driving margins. CORE RISK VERDICT. The base is a stable, cash-generative ~10–11% EBITDA-margin franchise with pricing power (it passed a 5x sulphur shock with ~5–6% hikes) — but it is structurally low-growth (3y CAGR 6%) and low single-digit-to-low-teens ROCE on a consolidated basis. It is a fine cash engine and a poor compounder. Owning it for the base alone does not justify ~39x; the base's job is to fund and survive while EES proves out.
