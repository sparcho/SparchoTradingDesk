L1 — 2-sentence plain-language summary

If Kevin Warsh is confirmed as Fed Chair on May 15, his stated views imply a US yield-curve steepener — dovish on the Fed Funds short-end (he sees AI as structurally deflationary), but anti-QE / pro-smaller-balance-sheet on the long end (which mechanically lifts US 10Y). For Indian markets, that mix matters more than "Warsh = cuts" because the long-end channel feeds INR carry, equity discount rates, silver real-yields, and bank NIM-vs-NII tension — all four moving in different directions if the steepener thesis plays out.

L2 — Structured findings (cheat-sheet)

What Warsh actually said (vs market caricature)

  • Public hearing 2026-04-22 (AP): would not commit to any specific rate decision; "I will be an independent actor"; emphasised inflation is "a choice."
  • Stated AI-deflation lens: "front end of a productivity boom" → can stay dovish on Fed Funds even if growth re-accelerates.
  • Anti-QE record (since 2008 governorship): consistent QE skeptic; pro-smaller-balance-sheet rhetoric in recent op-eds.
  • The paradox: dovish-on-short + hawkish-on-long = steepener, not parallel cuts.
  • Discipline reminder: the chair is one vote of twelve. Confirmation ≠ guaranteed policy shift.

Three most common mistakes / misconceptions

  1. "Warsh = guaranteed cuts." False. He's one vote of twelve, dovish only on the short end, and won't publicly commit per AP 260422. Pricing his confirmation as a single dovish event ignores the long-end story entirely.
  2. "Steepener helps everything risk-on." False. Steepener helps banks' NII but compresses high-PE growth names via discount-rate pressure. Bifurcated, not uniform.
  3. "AI-deflation thesis is settled." False. Warsh stated it; that doesn't make it true. If wrong, real yields don't rise as much, silver headwind softens, equity discount-rate pressure milder. The thesis depends on AI-deflation actually showing up in inflation prints — falsifiable.

L3 — First principles + alternatives + consensus map

First principles (Rule 3)

  1. Long-end rates are market-set, not Fed-set. Even an anti-QE chair can only nudge the long end via balance-sheet tools; the market still prices it. (Source: Saarbach/Kelly slide 6, Federal Reserve doctrine — primary.)
  2. Fed Chair = 1 of 12 votes. Confirmation ≠ committee shift. Voting math drives realised policy. (Source: FOMC composition — primary.)
  3. AI-deflation thesis is testable, not settled. Warsh's claim has a falsification test: do CPI/PCE prints actually decelerate beyond consensus over FY26-27? (Operator's domain to monitor.)
  4. Indian-market discount rates are anchored to US 10Y. No clean Indian risk-free benchmark used in DCF practice; 10Y is the input. (Convention; tertiary but operationally true.)
  5. Steepener vs flattener = different trades. A naïve "Fed cuts → risk-on" frame ignores that long-end-up makes some assets bearish (silver, high-PE names) even as short-end-down helps others (banks NII, near-term-cashflow names).
