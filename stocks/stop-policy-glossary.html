Stop & Policy Glossary

Plain-English reference for every term and stop-management policy used by the Stop-Policy Championship harness (stop_policy_championship) and the Trade Lab / Research panels of the equity dashboard. This is the single place to look up what "1R", "ratchet", "scratch", or trail_atr actually mean.

The championship is a measurement layer only — it replays every closed system trade under each policy below and scores which stop discipline would have made the most money over our own trade population, net of costs. It changes nothing about the live rule until a policy wins on n ≥ 30.

1. Core terms

R / risk-unit

R is one unit of risk — the rupee distance between your entry price and your original stop. If you buy at ₹100 with a stop at ₹95, then 1R = ₹5. Every outcome in the system is measured in R rather than rupees, so a small position and a large position are comparable: "+2R" means the trade made twice what it risked, whatever the position size. Costs are also expressed in R (a ~0.2% round-trip becomes a small fraction of an R and is subtracted from every result).

Full-risk stop (1R stop)

The original, rule-based stop placed at trade entry — the invalidation level. Being stopped here is a −1R loss (a full unit of risk), before costs. This is the widest the loss is ever allowed to be; every policy below only ever moves the stop *tighter* (up, for a long), never wider.

Breakeven (BE)

Your entry price. A stop "at breakeven" means the worst case is now a scratch (roughly zero, minus costs) instead of a loss. Moving a stop to BE removes downside risk but gives up nothing on the upside.

Profit-Lock-to-BE

The act of raising the stop from the full-risk (1R) level up to breakeven once the trade is comfortably ahead. It "locks in" the elimination of loss: from that point the trade can only scratch or win. This is the single move that defines the belock_r1 policy.

Live QUICK_EXIT Profit-Lock (the executed rule). The live resolver parks the stop at entry + cost_buffer (0.2%) — a hair above breakeven, so a scratch covers costs — and arms it when either a session CLOSES ≥ entry + 0.5R *or* (F135 high-arm) an intraday HIGH ≥ entry + 1.0R (PROFIT_LOCK_HIGH_R, tunable). The high-arm closes the *give-back* gap: a trade that spikes to +1R and fades the same day still locks the gain next session instead of riding the full original stop. Ratchets up only (monotonic), effective the next session.

Ratchet

A stop that moves in one direction only — never backward. For a long, a ratcheting stop only ever steps *up*; if price pulls back, the stop holds where it is. The mechanism behaves like a ratchet wrench: it clicks tighter and locks. Both trailing policies (trail_priorlow, trail_atr) ratchet.

Trail

A stop that follows price as it moves in your favour, keeping a chosen distance behind it. A trailing stop is always also a ratchet (it follows up, never down). The two trailing methods differ only in *what* they follow: the prior session's low (trail_priorlow) or a volatility band below the peak (trail_atr).

Scratch

A flat exit — roughly zero P&L, minus costs. Happens when a stop sitting at (or just above) breakeven is hit: the trade neither won nor lost meaningfully. "Scratch a stalled trade" = get out near flat rather than let a going-nowhere position bleed or round-trip a gain.

Stall Exit

A hard time limit: if a trade has hit neither its target nor its stop after a fixed number of sessions, it is force-closed at that session's close regardless of where price sits. It frees capital from trades that are going nowhere. This is the mechanism behind the stall_exit_k policy (current default k = 2, i.e. exit at the close of day 2). Distinct from the grace family, which only *tightens the stop* on a stall rather than force-closing.

Progress test (F135). Whether a held trade is "going nowhere" is judged on closing progress (STALL_PROGRESS_BASIS = "close"), not the intraday high. So a spike-and-fade — a new intraday high that closes back below the entry session's close — reads as *stalling*, and the day-N exit still fires. The old intraday-high test let a single wick silence the stall clock and keep a dead trade alive.

R1 milestone vs Trim-1 / Trim-2 / Trim-3

These are easily confused — they are different things:

  • R1 milestone (+1R) is a *measurement marker* used internally by the stop

policies: the price level one full risk-unit above entry (entry + 1R). Several policies "arm" when +1R prints (e.g. belock_r1 moves the stop to breakeven the session after +1R is tagged). +1R is not an exit — nothing is sold when it is reached; it is only the trigger that lets a stop start moving.

  • Trim-1 / Trim-2 / Trim-3 are *execution actions* on the live book — the

staged partial sells of an actual position as it runs into target zones (the exit ladder). They reduce position size; the R1 milestone does not.

In short: R1 is a trigger for the stop machinery; Trim-1/2/3 are real sells of shares. Do not read "+1R milestone" as "take the first trim."

2. The stop-management policies

Each policy is one stop-discipline the championship replays. The identifier (code) is the machine name; the label is what the dashboard shows.

baseline — *Fixed stop*

The stop never moves. The trade runs until it hits its target or falls back to the original full-risk (1R) stop. The simplest rule, and the control against which every other policy is scored.

belock_r1 — *Move to breakeven* (Profit-Lock-to-BE)

Once the trade is comfortably ahead — specifically, the session after +1R prints — the stop is raised to your entry price (breakeven). From then on the worst case is a scratch, not a loss. (Renamed from the older code be_after_t1: "BE-lock at the R1 milestone".)

grace_1 / grace_2 / grace_3 — *Breakeven if flat (the grace family)*

A patience window. If the trade is not in profit after k sessions (1, 2 or 3), the stop is pulled up to breakeven so a stalled trade scratches instead of bleeding — but a trade that *is* working keeps its full-risk room. More grace days = more time to start working before the stop tightens. (Renamed from the older code patience_k; tuned by the GRACE_K sweep in stop_policy_championship.)

trail_priorlow — *Trail under the lows*

Each session the stop is ratcheted up to just under the previous session's low — it follows price up like a fence and never moves down. The tightest of the trailing methods; gives back the least on a reversal but is the most easily shaken out. (Renamed from the older code bar_low_ratchet.)

trail_atr — *Trail below the peak* (chandelier)

The stop sits a fixed "normal daily wiggle" (an ATR multiple, default 3×ATR(14)) below the highest close reached, trailing up on new closing highs. Gives jumpy / volatile names more room than trail_priorlow. (Renamed from the older code atr_chandelier.)

stall_exit_2 — *Time exit (Stall Exit)*

A hard time limit: if neither target nor stop is hit, the trade is force-closed at the end of day 2 regardless of price. Frees capital from going-nowhere trades. The session count k is set by STALL_EXIT_K (default 2). (Renamed from the older code hard_scratch_k.) Unlike the grace family, which only tightens the stop on a stall, Stall Exit closes the position outright.
